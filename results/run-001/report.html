Retrieval Benchmark Baseline — Run 001

The first end-to-end, real-infrastructure comparison of HiveMind's graph-traversal retriever against a plain vector-RAG baseline and a lightweight GraphRAG baseline — across three corpus-growth checkpoints, judged by a real LLM. This run is the reference point every future run gets compared against.

2026-07-12 96 scored answers (32 queries × 3 arms) checkpoints 20% · 50% · 100% of 32-doc corpus llm + judge: OpenRouter / gpt-4o-mini spend: $0.1677 of $2.00 cap wall-clock: ~35 min

01 Executive summary

At full corpus size, vector_rag recalls nearly 3× more relevant passages than hivemind (0.823 vs 0.302), and edges it out slightly on judged answer quality. hivemind's advantage is precision — when it does retrieve, a much higher share of what it returns is actually relevant (0.719 vs 0.494). Both meaningfully beat graphrag_lite on every metric, at every checkpoint. Cost and per-call cost are indistinguishable across arms — the dominant real cost is the shared final-answer + judge calls, not the retrieval method.

04 The graph-expansion regression

Graph traversal expansion hurt precision at every checkpoint — with no exception.

hivemind's retriever expands past initial vector hits by walking graph edges (GraphNeighbors). This benchmark's own traversal-precision instrumentation compared precision with expansion on vs. off, and expansion was strictly worse all three times:

CheckpointPrecision, expansion onPrecision, expansion offΔ
20%0.1060.119−0.013
50%0.1130.161−0.048
100%0.1880.242−0.054

The gap widens as the corpus grows — at 100% docs, turning expansion off would have recovered ~29% relative precision. This is a direct, actionable lead: it suggests the traversal step is currently pulling in more noise than signal as the graph gets denser, worth investigating before trusting hivemind's graph-walk as a precision-improving mechanism.

05 Reading the results

vector_rag is the strongest arm on this corpus, on two of three axes.
It leads recall by a wide and growing margin (2.4× at 20%, widening to 2.7× at 100%) and edges out the highest judge score. Its retrieval step is also ~70× faster than hivemind's (0.05–0.06s vs. 2.5–4.7s) since it's a single embedding lookup with no downstream graph walk or LLM-driven intent refinement.
hivemind wins precision decisively, but only once the corpus is large.
At 20% corpus size hivemind's precision (0.188) trails both other arms — its intent-refinement/synthesis retrieval logic seemingly needs more graph structure to work with before it pays off. By 100% it more than doubles vector_rag's precision (0.719 vs 0.494), meaning what it does surface is highly likely to be relevant. Whether this precision edge justifies its recall gap and much higher latency depends on the target use case — high-precision citation tasks would favor hivemind; broad-coverage tasks favor vector_rag.
graphrag_lite underperforms both other arms at every checkpoint, on every metric.
Its recall and precision essentially plateau across corpus growth (0.188→0.260 recall, 0.113→0.156 precision) instead of scaling up the way vector_rag and hivemind do — worth checking whether this baseline's implementation has a ceiling bug (e.g. a fixed candidate limit) rather than a fundamental limitation of lightweight GraphRAG.
Real spend and per-call cost are effectively identical across arms.
$0.089–0.096 per 1,000 queries regardless of arm — the shared final-answer-generation and judge calls dominate spend, not the retrieval method itself. Total run cost: $0.1677 against a $2.00 cap. Cost is not a meaningful differentiator between these three arms at this scale.

06 Methodology & caveats

LLM (retrieval + final answer)
OpenRouter, openai/gpt-4o-mini (default model)
Judge
OpenRouter, openai/gpt-4o-mini — same model family as the generator
Corpus
32 synthetic documents, real ingestion (gRPC put_segment, real smokeserver engine)
Queries
32 labeled queries per checkpoint, evaluated identically across all 3 arms
k
Top-5 retrieval, top-5 candidates considered
Cost accounting
Every non-judge and judge call routed through one shared, fail-closed cost interceptor

What this baseline does not yet establish:

  • Single run, no repeated trials — no variance/confidence interval on any metric shown here. A metric that moved 0.02 between runs would be indistinguishable from noise.
  • Generator and judge share the same model family (gpt-4o-mini) — a same-family judge can systematically favor answer styles that model tends to produce; an independent judge model would strengthen confidence in the judge-score gap.
  • 32-document corpus is small; recall/precision dynamics (especially graphrag_lite's plateau) may not hold at production-scale corpora.
  • Latency figures are single-machine, single-run wall-clock — not representative of production infra or concurrent load.
